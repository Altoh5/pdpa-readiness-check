Malaysia PDPA 2025 Amendments

PDPA Compliance Documentation

A practical guide to Malaysia's Personal Data Protection Act amendments that took effect 1 June 2025 — covering who must comply, what is required, and how this tool helps.

What Changed on 1 June 2025
Already in force The PDPA (Amendment) Act 2024 introduced mandatory DPO appointment and breach notification requirements effective 1 June 2025. Enforcement is modelled on Singapore's PDPC framework.

Mandatory DPO Appointment

Any organisation that processes personal data of 20,000 or more individuals must appoint a Data Protection Officer. The DPO must be registered with the Personal Data Protection Department (PDPD) within 21 days of appointment. The DPO's contact details must also be published publicly on your website.

72-Hour Breach Notification to Commissioner

When a data breach is detected, organisations must notify the PDPD Commissioner within 72 hours of becoming aware. This window begins from the moment a responsible person in the organisation first knows about the breach — not when it is fully investigated.

7-Day Notification to Affected Individuals

If the breach is likely to cause harm to affected individuals, they must be notified within 7 calendar days. The notification must include the nature of the breach, the data affected, and steps the individual can take to protect themselves.

Who Must Comply
The 20,000 threshold PDPA applies to any data user (company or individual) in Malaysia that processes personal data in the course of commercial transactions. The mandatory DPO requirement kicks in at 20,000 data subjects.
  • Covered Any business registered in Malaysia processing personal data (customer lists, HR records, patient data, mailing lists) in a commercial context.
  • DPO Required If you hold records for 20,000+ individuals — regardless of data type. Sensitive data (health, financial, biometric) raises urgency at any volume.
  • Exemptions Federal/state governments and personal or domestic use are exempt. Third-party data processors acting solely on behalf of a data user follow the data user's obligations.
Breach Response Timeline

Hour 0 — Discovery

A responsible person in your organisation becomes aware of a suspected or confirmed breach. The 72-hour clock starts now — not after investigation is complete.

Within 72 Hours — Notify PDPD Commissioner

Submit a breach notification to the Commissioner with: nature of the breach, categories and approximate number of individuals affected, likely consequences, and measures taken or proposed.

Within 7 Days — Notify Affected Individuals

If the breach is likely to cause harm, affected individuals must be notified. Notification must be clear, in plain language, and include what steps they can take.

Non-compliance risk Failure to notify can result in fines of up to RM 500,000 and/or imprisonment. PDPD enforcement is modelled on Singapore's PDPC, which has issued multi-million dollar fines for notification failures.
About This Assessment Tool

How Scoring Works

The assessment has 7 questions with a maximum score of 11 points. Points are awarded based on the maturity of your controls — e.g., a tested breach response plan scores higher than an untested one. Scores map to three risk tiers: High Risk (0–35%), Partial Compliance (36–65%), and Good Baseline (66–100%).

Limitations

This tool provides an indicative readiness score — it is not legal advice and does not constitute a formal PDPA audit. Results depend on self-reported answers. A qualified DPO or legal counsel should conduct a formal gap analysis for compliance purposes.

Privacy

All assessment logic runs entirely in your browser. No answers are transmitted to any server. No account or signup is required. Nothing is stored.
